Discovery of the HMS Gloucester Wreck

After lying undiscovered for over 300 years beneath the waters off the Norfolk coast, the wreck of the HMS Gloucester was finally located in 2007 by a dedicated team of researchers, divers, and historians. The ship, which ran aground in 1682 while carrying the future King James II, became one of the most significant maritime finds in British history.

The Wreck of the Gloucester off Yarmouth, 6 May 1682, by Johan Danckerts, c. 1682

Key Moments In The Discovery

2007 – The Wreck Is Found!

After a four-year search that covered around 5,000 nautical miles, the wreck was located by Norfolk divers Julian and Lincoln Barnwell, along with their dive partner James Little. The team first spotted several large cannons on the seabed, which signalled a major wreck.

2012 – Confirmation

In 2012, the ship’s bell (dated 1681) was recovered. This confirmed 100% that the wreck was the HMS Gloucester. Additional discoveries such as clay pipes, bottles, navigational tools, and personal items proved this further.

2012–2021 – Research

Divers and archaeologists carried out extensive documentation of the site. Over 144 artefacts were recovered from two wooden chests, showing both personal and domestic items from life onboard. Institutions such as the Norfolk Museums Service and the University of East Anglia supported long-term conservation and research of the wreckage.

June 2022 – Public Announcement

The discovery was announced publicly after measures were taken to protect the site, as it lied in international waters, and complete initial archaeological work. Historians praised the wreck as one of the most significant maritime finds since the Mary Rose in 1982.

Wreck Discovery Site

The HMS Gloucester wreck was discovered around 28 miles off the Norfolk coast, lying partially buried in sand. Shifting seabeds and poor visibility made it extremely hard to locate for over 300 years.
